†† The four jh»nas - first, second, third, fourth - are explained in detail in the fourth chapter. That is the four jh»nas or five jh»nas that are attained through the practice of earth kasi”a meditation are explained.

†† You may remember that there are ten kasi”a objects for meditation mentioned earlier at the beginning of chapter four. Chapter five deals with the remaining kasi”a objects of meditation. We have the earth kasi”a in chapter four. In chapter five we will have the remaining nine kasi”as.

†† The next kasi”a is the water kasi”a. The word ëkasi”aí cannot be translated into English. It is very difficult. For the sake of convenience we translate it as disk meditation. Not all the kasi”a objects are disks however. So we will just leave it untranslated and use the word ëkasi”aí. ëKasi”aí means whole or orb. When you practice with the kasi”a, you take the whole of the object, not just part of the object. That is why it is called ëkasi”aí. In P»ƒi ëkasi”aí means whole or orb.

†† Now we want to practice water kasi”a. What should we do? ìOne who wants to develop the water kasi”a should, as in the case of the earth kasi”a, seat himself comfortably and apprehend the sign in water that is ëeither made up or not made upí.î Water kasi”a can be practiced without a made up kasi”a. One may just look at water and practice water kasi”a meditation on it. That is for those who had experience in the past, especially in past lives. For those who have not had previous experience, they will have to make a water kasi”a.

†† ìSomeone with no such previous practice should guard against the four faults of a kasi”a.î That means the water should be clear water or colorless. It should not be red or yellow, but it should be clear water. ëThe four faults of a kasi”aí means it should not be mixed with color. If it is mixed with color, it will become color kasi”a and not water kasi”a. The water has to be pure and clean.

†† ìHe should fill a bowl or a four-footed water pot (something like a kettle) to the brim with water uncontaminated by soil, taken in the open through a clean cloth [strainer], or with any other clear unturbid water. He should put it in a screened place on the outskirts of the monastery as already described and seat himself comfortably. He should neither review its color nor bring its characteristic to mind.î That means he should not reflect on its color because it will be color kasi”a and not water kasi”a. You should not reflect on the characteristic of water. Water has the characteristic of cohesion. Here you are to take water as a concept, not as a reality.

†† ìHe should neither review its color nor bring its characteristic to mind, apprehending the color as belonging to its physical support.î Clear is a kind of color. One should not pay attention to it although one may be seeing it. It is like the earth kasi”a. You may be seeing the earth or the color of the earth,† but you do not reflect on it. You do not keep your mind on the color of the earth but on the earth itself. In the same way here even though there may be some color in the water, you do not pay attention to color, but you pay attention to the substance called ëwaterí.

†† ìHe should set his mind on the [name] concept as the most outstanding mental datum, and using any among the [various] names for water (»po) such as rain (ambu), liquid (udaka), dew (v»ri), fluid (salila), he should develop [the kasi”a] by using [preferably] the obvious ëwater, waterí.î Then the footnote states: ìEnglish cannot really furnish five words for water.î In P»ƒi there are many synonyms for water. The author tells us you can say any of the words.

Student: You shouldnít switch words. You should just focus on one word.

Teacher: thatís right.

Student: The water should be very still?

Teacher: Yes.

†† In English we just say ëwater, waterí or you could say ëliquid, liquidí or you could say some other thing. ëWaterí is the word that comes to us naturally when we see the substance.

Student: so this is similar to mantra practice.

Teacher: It is something like that, but the meditator does not concentrate on the sound ëwaterí. He concentrates on the substance, on the concept. That is the difference.

†† Then he develops the kasi”a looking at the water and then closing his eyes trying to visualize it, and then he looks at the water again like the meditator did with the earth kasi”a. Then he gets first jh»na, second jh»na and so on as with the earth kasi”a.

†† Here the learning sign and the counterpart sign - ìIf the water has bubbles of froth mixed with it, the learning sign has the same appearance.î If there are bubbles of water, or froth, or whatever the learning sign is the same as that because the learning sign is the exact image of the real thing. After you get the learning sign† or the grasped sign, you may go to any place and sit down and concentrate on that visualized or memorized image. Then the faults in the learning sign will gradually disappear and it will become very clear.

†† ìThe learning sign has the same appearance, and it is evident as a fault in the kasi”a. But the counterpart sign appears inactive (That means still.), like a crystal fan set in space, like the disk of a looking-glass made of crystal. With the appearance of that sign he reaches access jh»na (That is neighborhood jh»na.) and the jh»na tetrad and pentad in the way already described.î He dwells upon the counterpart sign again and again and gets jh»na - first jh»na, second jh»na, third jh»na, fourth jh»na, or five jh»nas. That means if he eliminates vitakka and vic»ra at the same time, there are four jh»nas. If he eliminates the jh»na factors one at a time, then there are five jh»nas.

†† The remaining kasi”as are pretty much the same. With fire kasi”a you look at some fire. ìHerein, when someone with merit, having had previous practice, is apprehending the sign, it arises in him in any sort of fire, not made up, as he looks at the fiery combustion in a lampís flame or in a furnace, or in a place for baking bowls, or in a forest conflageration, as in the Elder Cittaguttaís case.î

†† ìThe sign arose as he was looking at a lampís flame while he was in the Uposatha house on the day of preaching the Dhamma. Anyone else should make one up.î If a person has no practice previously, he will have to make a fire kasi”a.

†† One should build a fire. ìHe should make a hole a span and four fingers wide in a rush mat or a piece of leather or a cloth.î You make a hole. It may be about ten inches in diameter. Then you look at the fire through that hole. You say ëfire, fire, fireí. There are different synonyms for fire. You may take any of those. In the same way the learning sign and the counterpart sign will appear to you. You dwell on the counterpart sign and jh»na will arise. Then first, second, third and fourth jh»na will come.

†† Then there is the air kasi”a. How do we practice the air kasi”a? A yogi should practice air kasi”a by looking at something or by feeling. When you look at a tree shaking in the wind, you see that that is wind. That is air. Or as it is said in this book you may sit near a window and feel the wind touching your body. You may concentrate on that feeling of air touching your body or the movement of air in the treetops. There are two ways that you can practice air kasi”a meditation. Then you can say any one of the synonyms for wind or air. In English it is just air.

†† ìHere the learning sign appears to move like the swirl of hot [steam] on rice gruel just withdrawn from an oven. The counterpart sign is quiet and motionless.î The counterpart sign is always like that. It is more refined, smoother, and maybe more brilliant, but the learning sign is just the exact image of the object.

†† Next is the blue kasi”a. The P»ƒi word for blue is n la. This word means more than just blue. For example it can mean black. Hair is said to be n la in color. Maybe not the color of the hair of Western people. I see many hair colors here. It is like the hair of Eastern people, black. In Burma we translate n la not as blue but as brown. Between brown and black we translate as n la. A lotus which is called ëblue lotusí in Burmese we call it ëbrown lotusí although it is blue.

Student: Could it be green?

Teacher: Yes, if it is very dark. So here we have any color bordering on blue - blue, or maybe dark green, or something like black.

†† ìOne who is learning the blue kasi”a apprehends the sign in blue, whether in a flower, or in a cloth, or in a color element.î ëColor elementí simply means paint. You may paint on a board blue color and practice on it.

†† ìOne should take flowers such as blue lotuses, girika””ik» (morning glory) flowers, etc., and spread them out to fill a tray or a flat basket completely so that no stamen or stalk shows, or with only their petals. Or he can fill it with blue cloth bunched up together; or he can fasten the cloth over the rim of the tray or basket like the covering of a drum. Or he can make a kasi”a disk, either portable as described under the earth kasi”a or on a wall, with one of the color elements such as bronze-green, leaf-green, anjana-ointment black.î Anjana-ointment is the cosmetic women use to decorate their eyes. What do you call that?

Students: Eye shadow. Mascara.

Teacher: Yes, mascara. It can be any color?

Student: Itís usually dark.

Teacher: Yes, it must be dark. So any one of these colors will do. He surrounds it with a different color. Let us say you want to make a blue kasi”a. The circle should be blue and the bordering color should not be red, yellow or white, but some other color. There should be no confusion with the colors of the other kasi”as. ìAfter that he should bring it to mind as ëblue, blueí.î He looks at it and says ëblue, blueí. The rrest is similar to the preceding kasi”a objects.

†† With the yellow kasi”a one uses a yellow cloth, yellow flowers or yellow paint. The only difference is the color.

†† With the red kasi”a you use red color - red flowers, red cloth or red paint. With white kasi”a you use white flowers, white cloth or white paint.

†† Next is the light kasi”a. ìOne who is learning the light kasi”a apprehends the sign in light in a hole in a wall, or in a keyhole, or in a window opening.î So it is light coming through a hole.

†† ìSo firstly, when someone has merit, having had previous practice, the sign arises in him when he sees the circle thrown on a wall or a floor by sunlight or moonlight entering through a hole in the wall, etc., or when he sees a circle thrown on the ground by sunlight or moonlight coming through a gap in the branches of a dense-leafed tree or through a gap in a hut made of closely packed branches. Anyone else should use that same kind of circle of luminosity just described, developing it as ëluminosity, luminosity, or ëlight, lightí. If he cannot do so, he can light a lamp inside a pot, close the potís mouth, make a hole in it and place it with the hole facing a wall. The lamplight coming out of the hole throws a circle on the wall.î He looks at that circle and says ëlight, lightí.

†† ìThis lasts longer than the other kinds.î That means that sunlight or moonlight may change. They may not last long, but this light which is put in a pot and that falls on the wall will last longer.

†† ìThe learning sign is like the circle thrown on the wall or the ground. The counterpart sign is like a compact bright cluster of lights. The rest is as before.î

†† The last one is the limited space kasi”a. Here ëlimited spaceí is said to differentiate it from ëinfinite spaceí which is the object of the first formless (ar|p»vacara) jh»na. After getting the fifth or the fourth jh»na, if a person wants to attain ar|p»vacara jh»na, first he has to practice the kasi”a meditation. Then he makes the sign disappear and he dwells on the space that was taken by the sign. Then he expands this sign until the end of the world or the end of the universe.

†† But here the yogi takes the space which is limited. It is like looking at a round hole or something like that. ìOne who is learning the space kasi”a apprehends the sign in a hole in a wall, or in a keyhole, or in a window opening, and so firstly, when someone has merit, having had previous practice, the sign arises in him when he sees any [such gap as a] hole in a wall. Anyone else should make hole a span and four fingers broad in a well thatched hut, or in a piece of leather, or in a rush mat, and so on. He should develop one of these, or a hole such as a hole in a wall, as ëspace, spaceí.î So he looks at the hole and says ëspace, spaceí.

†† ìHere the learning sign resembles the hole together with the wall.î So there is a limit. There is a boundary. That is why in that state attempts to expand it fail. You cannot expand that in the mind because it has a limited boundary.

†† ìThe counterpart sign appears only as a circle of space.î Here there is no boundary, just a circle of space, and so attempts to extend it succeed. ìThe rest should be understood as described under the earth kasi”a.î

†† These are the ten ksi”as. All of the ten kasi”as lead a yogi to attain all four jh»nas or all five jh»nas.

†† Then by defilement - that means those who have fixed wrong view or who are hermaphrodites or eunuchs. These people cannot attain jh»na even though they practice meditation. ëWrong viewí means no cause view, moral inefficacy of action, and nihilist view. ëNo cause viewí means there is no cause for something to happen. Anything can happen without a cause. That is called ëno cause viewí. If you deny the cause, you deny the effect. In reality these three kinds of wrong view are the same.

†† The others deny the effect. If you deny the effect, you also deny the cause. Then third one is the nihilist view. There is nothing. Whatever you do will not constitute a kamma. The roots of good are the opposites of these three. When a person takes rebirth, his rebirth consciousness may not be accompanied by any of these roots at all or it may be accompanied by two or three of the good roots.
