The last chapter is a description of the benefits in developing understanding. That means the benefits of meditation.

 

Student: I want to note something. Most people when they come want to know what they can get out of meditation. In America this should be the first chapter instead of the last chapter.

Removal of the various defilements, experience of the taste of cessation, ability to attain the attainment of cessation, and achievement of worthiness to receive gifts and so on. There are four benefits to be derived from the practice of meditation, especially the practice of vipassan» meditation. I say ëespecially vipassan»í because it says developing understanding or wisdom.

Paragraph 3† Next is the experience of the taste of the Noble Fruit. Now after Path follows Fruition immediately. ìNot only the removal of the various defilements but also the experience of the taste of the Noble Fruit is a benefit of the development of understanding. For it is the fruitions of Stream Entry, etc., - the Fruits of Asceticism -, that are called the ëNoble Fruití.î The Noble Fruits or the Four Fruitions are called ëthe Fruits of Asceticismí or ëthe Fruits of Monkhoodí. In P»ƒi they are called ës»ma““a-phalaí. There is a discourse by that name, ëS»ma““aphala Suttaí, in the D gha Nik»ya (The Collection of Long Discourses). Paragraph 5 ìHowever, in order to show how it occurs in the attainment of Fruition there is the following set of questions.î According to these questions he will explain: (1) What is Fruition attainment?, (2) Who attains it?, (3) Who do not attain it?, (4) Why do they attain it?, (5) How does its attainment come about?, (6) How is it made to last?, (7) How does the emergence from it come about?, (8) What is next to Fruition?, (9) What is Fruition next to?

 

The first question is: ìWhat is Fruition attainment? It is absorption in the cessation in which the Noble Fruition consists.î I donít think this translation is correct. Actually what it means is: ìIt is absorption of the Noble Fruit in the cessation.î ëCessationí here means Nibb»na. So it is the absorption of the Noble Fruition in Nibb»na. That means Noble Fruition is arising again and again taking Nibb»na as object. ìIt is absorption of the Noble Fruition in the cessation.î We may put the word ëNibb»naí after cessation in parenthesis.

 

ìWho attains it? Who do not attain it? No ordinary men attain it. Why? Because it is beyond their reach. But all Noble Ones attain it. Why? Because it is within their reach. But those who have reached a higher Path do not attain a lower Fruition (That means they do not enter the lower Fruition attainment.) because the state of each successive Person is more tranquilized than the one below.î Actually it means when you reach the second stage, the first stage disappears. It is something like that. ëThey are tranquilizedí really means ëdisappearanceí. When you get to the Second Path, then there is no First Path. ìAnd those who have only reached a lower Path do not attain a higher Fruition because it is beyond their reach.î So if you have attained only the First Magga, you can go into First Fruition, but not the second stage of Fruition, or the third stage of Fruition and so on. Then when you have reached the second stage, you can enter into the second stage of Fruition. You cannot enter into the first stage of Fruition because it has already gone and the third and the fourth you have not yet attained. So you can enter into the second stage only. If you have reached the third stage, you can enter into the third stage of Fruition only, not the first nor the second because they have disappeared, and not the fourth because you have not yet attained it. ìBut each one attains his appropriate Fruition.î We may say that Sot»panna can attain only first Fruition attainment, the Once-Returner can only attain the second Fruition attainment and so on.

 

Paragraph 7 ìBut there are some who say that the Stream Enterer and Once-Returner do not attain it, and that only the two above them attain it.î This is the opinion of some teachers. ìThe reason they give is that only these two show achievement in concentration.î It is said that only when a person who has reached the third stage of enlightenment has he really achieved concentration. These teachers think that these two cannot enter into Fruition attainment. ìBut that is no reason, since even the ordinary man attains such mundane concentration as is within his reach.î So if even puthujjanas can reach the jh»nas and enter into jh»na attainment, why are the Stream Enterer and Once-Returner not able to go into their respective attainments? Therefore, their reason is no reason at all. ìBut why argue here over what is and what is not a reason? Is it not said in the Text as follows?î And then he brings out the Text. We will skip over it.

 

Paragraph 8 ìWhy do they attain it? For the purpose of abiding in bliss here and now.î That means in modern terms to enjoy themselves or to enjoy the bliss of Nibb»na here and now. ìFor just as a king experiences royal bliss and a deity experiences divine bliss, so too the Noble Ones think ëWe shall experience the noble supramundane bliss, and after deciding on the duration, they attain the attainment of Fruition whenever they choose.î

 

In footnote 3† ìAlthough they are resultant statesî - now during the Fruition attainment, the Fruition consciousness alone arises, perhaps millions of times. Fruition consciousness is resultant consciousness. Right? With regard to other resultant consciousness do we have choice? We have no choice. It is the fruit of past kamma. So we have to just accept it. We see something good and there is eye consciousness. Right? We cannot avoid that. We have to accept it. But with regard to this resultant consciousness, Fruition consciousness, there is a choice. ìAlthough they are resultant states, nevertheless the states of Fruition attainment occur in the Noble Person only when he chooses since they do not arise without the preliminary work and do so only when they are given predominance.î So when they want to go into Fruition attainment, they have to practice. They have to do something. They have to practice vipassan» meditation again. They do not arise without preliminary work. That is why they are said to be ëwith predominanceí. That means they belong to javanas. The Fruition consciousness has the javana function. And then there is what is called ëpredominance (adhipati)í. When they arise, they have this predominance in them. What are the four adhipati? (1) desire (chanda), (2) consciousness (citta), (3) effort (viriya), (4) investigation (vima—s» or pa““»). You can find these four in The Manual of Abhidhamma. So I think the last part of the translation is not so correct† - ëthey do so only when they are given predominance.í Actually when the Fruition consciousness arises, one of these four has the function of predominance. One will always have predominance. And they arise only with preliminary work. That is why, it is said in the Visuddhi Magga ëwhenever they chooseí. It is different from other types of resultant consciousness.

 

Paragraph 9 ìHow does its attainment come about? How is it made to last?î That means how does it last? ìHow does the emergence from it come about? Paragraph 10 ìNow the process of attaining it is as follows.î It describes how to enter into Fruition attainment. ìA Noble Disciple who seeks the attainment of Fruition should go into solitary retreat.î That means he should go to a place where he has seclusion. ìHe should see formations with insight according to rise and fall and so on.î That means he must practice vipassan» meditation and see the rise and fall of mental and physical phenomena and then he must go up stage by stage of vipassan» knowledge. ìWhen that insight has progressed [as far as conformity], then comes change-of-lineage knowledge with formations as its object. There is footnote 4. Right? ìThen comes change-of-lineage knowledge with formations as its object.î Change-of-lineage is what? The P»ƒi is gotrabhu. In the Path process change-of-lineage takes what as object? It takes Nibb»na as object. But in this thought process it takes formations as object, not Nibb»na. ìWhy does change-of-lineage not have Nibb»na as its object here as it does when it precedes the Path?î In the Path thought process change-of-lineage takes Nibb»na as object. Why does it not take it here? ìBecause states belonging to Fruition are not associated with an outlet [as in the case of the Path].î ëOutletí means ëgetting you out of sa—s»ra or out of mental and physical phenomena. That means something that takes something out of something. ìHere it is only Fruition, not Path, that arises even in a Trainer.î ëTrainerí means a person who is enlightened, but who is not yet an Arahant. So such persons are called Trainersí. So even in a Trainer, Path consciousness does not arise because his tendency or his intention is to Fruition attainment. His aim is not to get Path consciousness but to get into Fruition attainment. That is why Path does not arise although he practices vipassan» meditation and he progresses through the successive stages of vipassan» knowledges, maybe even in one sitting. Path consciousness does not arise because his intention is not to get Path consciousness.

That means ëattached toí, ëattached to oneí, or ëconnected to oneí. ëNanabhaddhaí means the opposite of that. So the real meaning is the property that is not physically attached to him.

 

In paragraph 35 this ìrefers to ëwhat the bhikkhu has about him that is not his personal property: (No. That is wrong.) a robe and bowl, or a bed and chair, or a living room, or any other kind of requisite kept by him but the property of various others.î No. It is other kinds of requisites that lie unattached to him. When he enters into this attainment, he has his robe. Right? He has worn his robe. If he has a bowl and puts it to his left, he may have that bowl too. They are connected or attached to his body. He may have some other sets of robes or some other things, not with him or not attached physically to him. For those things he must do something so that they do not get damaged by fire, by water, by bites of rats and so on. So the first is non-damage to the property that is not attached to him, his property, not the property of others. If it were possible for othersí property to be like insured, we need such a monk, only one such monk. ìIt should be resolved that such property will not be damaged, will not be destroyed by fire, water, wind, thieves, rats, and so on. Here is the form of the resolve: (he makes the resolution in this way.) ëDuring these seven days let this and this not be burned by fire; let it not be swept off by water; let it not be spoilt by wind; let it not be stolen by thieves; let it not be devoured by rats, and so oní. When he has resolved in this way, they are not in danger these seven days.î That means for as long as he is in the attainment.

 

Paragraph 36 ìIf he does not resolve in this way, they may be destroyed by fire, etc., as in the case of the Elder Mah»-N»ga. He went for alms into the village where his mother, a lay follower, lived. She gave him rice gruel and seated him in the sitting hall. The Elder sat down and attained cessation.î So he entered into the cessation attainment in the village, in the sitting hall. ìwhile he was sitting there, the hall caught fire. The other bhikkhus each picked up their seats and fled. The villagers gathered together, and seeing the Elder, they said ëWhat a lazy monk!í The fire burned the grass thatch, the bamboos and timbers, and it encircled the Elder.î The fire did not burn the Elder, but everything else was burned. ìPeople brought water and put it out. They removed the ashes, did the repairs (that means like making the ground smooth again and so on.), scattered flowers, and then stood respectfully waiting. The Elder emerged at the time he had determined. Seeing them, he said ëI am discovered!í (Because he didnít want the people to know that he was an An»g»mi or an Arahant.) And he rose up into the air and went to Piya~gu Island.î So ìThis is no-damage to property (not ëothersí propertyí) that is not attached to him.î

 

Paragraph 37 ìThere is no special resolving to be done for what is his own personal property.î That means what is attached to his body, not ëhis own personal propertyí. For the robe he is wearing, he doesnít have to bother. He doesnít have to worry about that. He doesnít have to make a resolution for the safety of the robe he is wearing. It will be taken care of. There is no special resolving to be done for what is attached to his body such as the inner and outer robes or the seat he is sitting on. ìHe protects all that by means of the attainment itself, like those of the Venerable Sa“j va. And this is said ëThere was success by intervention of concentration in the Venerable Sa“j va. There was success by intervention of concentration in the Venerable S»riputta.î So you go back to chapter 12, paragraph 30. When you see that, you will know. Venerable Sa“j va was in attainment and people didnít know that he was a monk and so the people put fuel and wood on him and then set it on fire. But he was not burned because he was in attainment.

 

Paragraph 38 ìThe Communityís (or Sa~ghaís) waiting is the Communityís (Sa~ghaís) expecting; the meaning is: till this bhikkhu comes there is no carrying out of acts of the Community (Sa~gha).î When the Sa~gha does what we call ëthe acts of Sa~ghaí, then all monks must assemble to a place. If one monk is absent or away from the assembled monks, then that act becomes invalid. So he must come to where the other monks are assembled. But when he is in attainment, he may not be able to come. So he must do something about that. ìAnd here it is not the actual Communityís waiting that is the preparatory task, but the adverting to the waiting.î That means thinking of the waiting. ìSo it should be adverted to in this way ëWhile I am sitting for seven days in the attainment of cessation, if the Community wants to enact a resolution, etc., I shall emerge before any bhikkhu comes to summon meí.î So if the Sa~gha expects him, then he will emerge from the attainment. He must resolve like that.

 

Paragraph 39 ìBut if he does not do so, then perhaps the Community assembles, and not seeing him, it is asked ëWhere is the bhikkhu?î and so on. If he does not make the resolution before entering the attainment, then he will not emerge from it automatically. But he will emerge from it when a monk goes to him and just says to him ëYou are expected by the Sa~ghaí. Then he will surely emerge from the attainment.

 

Paragraph 40 Then the third one is the Masterís summons. ëMasterí means the Buddha. If Buddha wants him to come, then he must go to the Buddha and so on. It is similar to Communityís waiting.

 

Paragraph 42 The fourth one is the limit of life duration. So he must think of how long he will live before he enters into this attainment. At least he must know that he is going to live more than seven days. Otherwise there will be a dilemma. That is because during the attainment death cannot come. So he must surely make this preparatory task although the others can be ignored. So he should attain only after adverting to that. ìFor it is said that while it may be permissible to omit adverting to others (the other three), this however must be adverted to.î

 

ìNow when he has thus the base consisting of nothingness and done this preparatory task, he then attains the base consisting of neither perception nor non-perception. Then after one, or two, turns of consciousness have passed (That means one or two moments of the fourth ar|p»vacara jh»na.), he becomes without consciousness.î So consciousness ceases at that moment. He achieves cessation. There are no cittas, no cetasikas, no r|pa-born-citta. ìBut why do consciousnesses not go on occurring in him after the two consciousnesses? Because the effort is directed to cessation.î His effort is directed to cessation, so there is cessation of consciousness. ìFor the bhikkhuís mounting through the eight attainments, coupling together the states of serenity and insight is directed to successive cessation, not to attaining the base consisting of neither perception nor non-perception. So it is because the effort is directed to cessation that o more than two consciousnesses occur.î So there are not many moments of consciousness; only two consciousnesses occur.

 

Paragraph 44 ìBut if a bhikkhu emerges from the base consisting of nothingness without having done this preparatory task and then attains the base consisting of neither perception nor non-perception, he is unable to become without consciousness: he returns to the base consisting of nothingness and settles down there.î So he will not be able to make it disappear; he will go back to the third one.

 

Paragraph 48 ìHow is it made to last? It lasts as long as the time predetermined for its duration, unless interrupted meanwhile by the exhaustion of the life span, by the waiting of the Community, or by the Masterís summons.î

 

Paragraph 49 ìHow does the emergence from it come about? The emergence comes about in two ways thus: by means of the Fruition of non-return in the case of the Non-Returner, or by means of the Fruition of Arahantship in the case of the Arahant.î
