†† Today we come to the third chapter. With this chapter we begin the second stage of spiritual development, the stage of sam»dhi or concentration. The first chapter deals with the first stage, s la. †† The third chapter begins with the definition of the word ësam»dhií. It is important that we understand or learn what we are talking about. The word ësam»dhií is translated as concentration. In order to understand properly we have to go back to the P»ƒi word ësam»dhií. The word ësam»dhií is composed of ësamí, ë»í, and ëdh», plus ëií. The prefix ësamí here has two meanings. The first meaning is evenly. That means in a unifying way, without scattering. The second meaning is rightly. That means without being distracted. ëåí and ëdh»í together mean putting or placing. So sam»dhi means placing without scattering or placing, keeping them unified and undistracted.

††† Placing what? Placing the consciousness and its concomitants or the mental factors. Placing on what? Placing on a single object. Sam»dhi is a mental factor. It is one of the 52 mental factors. Among the 52 mental factors we call it ëone-pointedness of mindí or ëekaggat»í. Ekaggat» and sam»dhi are the same or synonymous. Sam»dhi is that mental factor which puts consciousness and its concomitants on a single object, without scattering them, keeping them unified and undistracted. Sam»dhi coordinates consciousness and all the mental factors arising with it and does not allow them or itself to be distracted or to wander away.

†† In another chapter of the Visuddhi Magga sam»dhi is described as the steadiness of mind, like the steadiness of a lampís flame when there is no draft. In a room with no draft there might be a candle with a flame. Like that flame sam»dhi is steady.

†† The steadiness of sam»dhi is a series of moments of steadiness. It is not one moment only. It is a series of moments of consciousness arising again and again on a single object. Sam»dhi is a mental factor and as a mental factor it must arise and disappear in just three sub-moments. So here ëbeing on the object for some time without scattering, unified and undisturbedí means a series of sam»dhi moments arising and disappearing, taking one and the same object, taking a single object. That is what is called ësam»dhií in Buddhism.

†† Whenever we want to understand something, we have to understand it with reference to characteristic, function, manifestation, and proximate cause. These are given in paragraph 4.î Concentration has non-distraction as its characteristic.î The characteristic of sam»dhi is non-distraction, non-wandering. ìIts function is to eliminate distraction. It is manifested as non-wavering (non-shaking). Its proximate cause is bliss.î ëBlissí means sukha, comfort or something like happiness. Only when there is something like comfort of the mind and the body can there be concentration or sam»dhi.

†† In another chapter characteristic and the others are given a little differently, although they mean more or less the same thing. In that chapter the characteristic is given as non-wandering or non-distraction. The function is to conglomerate conascent states as water does bath powder. That means you have some powder or flour and you put some water in it. The water conglomerates them into one solid thing. As water conglomerates the particles of powder, sam»dhi conglomerates, or coordinates, or collects concomitants. It does not let the concomitants (both citta and cetasikas) arising with it to scatter. It keeps together consciousness and mental factors. It does not let them be scattered. Its manifestation there is described as peace because when there is sam»dhi there is peacefulness. The proximate cause again is sukha (bliss).
