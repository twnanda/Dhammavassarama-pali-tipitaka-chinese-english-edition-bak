The Way to the Beyond
(Pàràyana)

Introductory Verses

1 (976)
From the delightful city of the Kosalans to the Southern lands went one who wanted to have no possessions, a brahmin perfect in lore.

2 (977)
In the locality of Assaka, and near to Måëaka close to the bank of the Godhàvari he lived on gleanings and fruit.

3 (978)
In the vicinity (of the river) there was a large village, and with the income that arose from that he offered a great sacrifice.

4 (979)
When that great sacrifice had been given he entered his hermitage again, and when he had re-entered it, another brahmin came along -

5 (980)
he had sore feet, and was thirsty, with dirty teeth, and dust on his head - and after approaching (the first brahmin), he begged for five hundred (coins).

6 (981)
After seeing him Bàvari invited him to take a seat, and he asked after his happiness and welfare, and this is the word he spoke:

7 (982)
ßWhatever kind of gift I had, all that has been given away by me, please excuse me brahmin, I do not have the five hundred.û

8 (983)
ßIf your honour will not give me what I am begging for, then within seven days may your head split into seven pieces!û

9 (984)
Having made (a scene) the dishonest man proclaimed this fearful thing. After hearing this word of his, Bàvari became miserable.

10 (985)
(Going) without food he dried up, and was affected by the dart of grief, and when his heart was like that his mind did not delight in meditation.

11 (986)
Having seen him terrified and miserable, a god who desired his welfare, approached Bàvari, and this is the word he spoke:

12 (987)
ßHe does not know the head - he is a dishonest man who wants wealth! He does not have knowledge about the head or head-splitting.û

13 (988)
ßBut now your honour knows! Please explain this to me when asked: the head and head-splitting, may we hear that word of yours.û

14 (989)
ßI also do not know about this, I do not have this knowledge here. The head and head-splitting! Indeed only Victors see this!û

15 (990)
ßThen who knows about the head and head-splitting on the face of the earth? O god, please explain this to me.û

16 (991)
ßFrom out of the city of Kapilavatthu a world leader has renounced, he is of king Okkàka's line, a Sakyan's son, a light-maker.

17 (992)
He is a Sambuddha, brahmin, in everything he is perfect, having attained all deep knowledges and strengths, endowed with Vision regarding all things, he has come to the end of all actions, in the end of all cleaving he is freed.

18 (993)
He is the Buddha in the world, the Auspicious One, the Visionary who teaches Dhamma, after going there you can ask him about it, he will explain it to you.û

19 (994)
Having heard the word `Sambuddha', Bàvari became elated, then his grief became but little, and he received great happiness.

20 (995)
Bàvari, uplifted and elated, enthusiastically asked that god: ßIn which village, or again in which town, in which country, is the lord of the world? Where having gone to can we revere the Sambuddha, the supreme human being?û

21 (996)
ßThe Victor is in the Kosalan's city, Sàvatthi, he has much wisdom, excellent and great intelligence, that Sakyan's son, who is free from burden and pollutant, that bull of a man has understanding of head-splitting.û

22 (997)
So then the brahmin addressed his students, who were perfect in the lore: ßCome, young men, and I shall explain, please listen to this word of mine:

23 (998)
He whose manifestation in the world is always exceptional, has now arisen in the world, renowned as one called a `Sambuddha'. Having gone quickly to Sàvatthi, you can see the supreme human being.û

24 (999)
ßHaving seen him how will we know that he is the `Buddha', O brahmin? You must tell us who do not know, how we can know it is him.û

25 (1000)
ßThere has come down to us in the lore the marks of a Great Man, they are explained as thirty-two, complete in regular order.

26 (1001)
For whoever has on his limbs the marks of a Great Man, there can be just two destinies, for a third cannot be found:

27 (1002)
If he lives in a house, when he has been victorious over this earth, without a stick or a sword he will rule according to the Dhamma.

28 (1003)
But if he goes forth from the house to the houseless life, he becomes a cover-remover, a Sambuddha, a Worthy One, unexcelled.

29 (1004)
About my birth, family, and marks; lore, students and other things, about the head and head-splitting, you must ask in your mind.

30 (1005)
If he should be a Buddha, one who sees without obstruction, the questions that are asked in your mind he will answer by way of speech.û

31 (1006)
After hearing Bàvari's words, the sixteen brahmin students: Ajita, Tissa Metteyya, Puõõaka, also Mettagå,

32 (1007)
Dhotaka, and Upasãva, Nanda, and also Hemaka, the two: Todeyya and Kappa, and Jatukaõõã, the one who is wise,

33 (1008)
Bhadràvudha and Udaya, and also the brahmin Posàla, Mogharàja, the intelligent one, and the great seer, Pingiya,

34 (1009)
each one with his own group, renowned throughout the whole world, meditators delighting in meditation, wise ones, influenced by their pre-dispositions,

35 (1010)
after worshipping Bàvari, and circumambulating him, they all, wearing matted-hair and deer-skins, set out for the North:

36 (1011)
To Patiññhàna of Måëaka first, then on to Mahissati, to Ujjeni, and Gonaddha, to Vedisa, and to the place called Vanasa,

37 (1012)
to Kosambi, and Sàketa, and Sàvathi, the city supreme, to Setabya, and Kapilavatthu, and to the city of Kusinàra,

38 (1013)
to Pàva, the Bhoga's fortified town, to Vesàli, and to the Magadhan city, and to the Pàsàõaka shrine, delightful, it is the mind's delight.

39 (1014)
Just like a thirsty man to cool water, or like a trader to great gain, like one scorched by the heat to shade, quickly he climbed the mountain.

40 (1015)
Now at that time the Auspicious One was in front of the bhikkhu Sangha, teaching the Dhamma to the bhikkhus, roaring like a lion in a forest,

41 (1016)
Ajita saw the Sambuddha, brilliant like the hundred-rayed sun, like the moon which has reached fullness on the fifteenth day of the month,

42 (1017)
and after seeing on his limbs the characteristics in their fullness, standing cheerfully on one side he asked a question in his mind:

43 (1018)
ßSpeak and point out his birth, speak of his family, and marks, speak of his perfection in lore, how many does the brahmin school?û

44 (1019)
ßOne hundred and twenty years his age, his family is Bàvari, three marks are on his limbs, he is perfect in the three Vedas.

45 (1020)
In the marks, and the traditions, with the glossaries, and the ritual, he schools five hundred, in his own teaching he is perfect.û

46 (1021)
ß(Your) investigation of Bàvari's marks, O one supreme among men, O craving-cutter, make that clear, so that we are not left doubting.û

47 (1022)
ßHe can cover his face with his tongue, there is hair between his eyebrows, what is covered by a cloth is ensheathed: know it is so young man.û

48 (1023)
Not hearing any question, but having heard the questions answered, all the people were enthusiastic, and raised their hands, thinking:

49 (1024)
ßNow who was it, a god, or a Brahma, or Inda, the lord of Sujà, who asked those questions with the mind? To whom did he make reply?

50 (1025)
ßAbout the head and head-splitting, Bàvari asks (this question), please explain that O Auspicious One, please remove our doubts, O seer.û

51 (1026)
"Know ignorance is called the head, and understanding is the head-splitter, joined with confidence, mindfulness, concentration, desire, and energy.û

52 (1027)
Then with great enthusiasm, being resolute, the young man, having put his deer-skin on one shoulder, fell placing his head at (the Buddha's) feet:

53 (1028)
ßThe honourable brahmin Bàvari, and his students, dear Sir, elated in heart, and happy in mind, worships your feet, O Visionary One.û

54 (1029)
ßMay the brahmin Bàvari, together with his students, be happy! And may you also be happy! May you live for a long time, young man!

55 (1030)
All of the doubts that Bàvari, or you, or anyone else has, having received leave, ask whatever you long (to know) in the mind.û

56 (1031)
Having received leave from the Sambuddha, sitting with hands raised in reverence, Ajita asked the Realised One the first question in that place:

The Introductory Verses are Finished

 

The Young Man Ajita's Questions

 

57 (1032) 11
ßBy what is the world enveloped? said venerable Ajita, Why does it not become clear? What do you say is its defilement? What is (the world's) great fear?û

58 (1033) 12
ßThe world is enveloped by ignorance, Ajita, said the Auspicious One, because of heedlessness and meanness it does not become clear. Hunger is its defilement, I say; suffering is (the world's) great fear.û

59 (1034) 13
ßStreams are flowing everywhere, said venerable Ajita, what is the constraint for streams? Tell me the restraint for streams; by what are the streams shut off?û

60 (1035) 14
ßWhatever streams there are in the world, Ajita, said the Auspicious One, mindfulness is the constraint for them. That is the restraint for streams, I say; by wisdom they are shut off.û

61 (1036) 15
ßWisdom and also mindfulness, said venerable Ajita, and name-and-form, dear Sir, please tell me this when asked: where does this cease?û

62 (1037) 16
ßThis question that was asked, Ajita, I can answer it! As to where name-and-form ceases without remainder: with the cessation of consciousness, in this place it ceases.û

63 (1038) 17
ßThose who have discerned the Dhamma, and the many in training here, when I ask the prudent one, please tell me their conduct, dear Sir.û

64 (1039) 18
ßHe should not be greedy for sense pleasures, or be disturbed in mind. Skilful in all things, the bhikkhu should wander mindfully.û

The Young Man Ajita's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Tissa Metteyya's Questions

 

65 (1040) 21
ßWho is satisfied here in the world? said venerable Tissa Metteyya, For whom is there no turmoil? Who is the wise man, who has known both ends, and is undefiled in the middle? Who do you say is a Great Man? Who has gone beyond the seamstress here?û

66 (1041) 22
ßHe who is chaste in regard to sense pleasures, Metteyya, said the Auspicious One, free from craving, always mindful, the bhikkhu who has discerned (the Dhamma) is still, for him there is no turmoil.

67 (1042) 23
He is the wise man, who has known both ends, and is undefiled in the middle. He, I say, is a Great Man, he has gone beyond the seamstress here.û

The Young Man Tissa Metteyya's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Puõõaka's Questions

 

68 (1043) 31
ßTo the one who is unmoved, who sees the root, said venerable Puõõaka, I have come in need with a question: On account of what did the seers and men, nobles, and brahmins, here in the world offer many sacrifices to the gods? I ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me.û

69 (1044) 32
ßWhoever of those seers and men, Puõõaka, said the Auspicious One, nobles, and brahmins, here in the world, offered many sacrifices to the gods, they did so yearning, Puõõaka, for mundane existence, on account of old age, they offered sacrifices.û

70 (1045) 33
ßWhoever of those seers and men, said venerable Puõõaka, nobles, and brahmins, here in the world offered many sacrifices to the gods, O Auspicious One, perhaps those heedful of the path of sacrifice have crossed over birth and old age, dear Sir? I ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me.û

71 (1046) 34
ßThey yearn and they praise, they hunger, and they offer, said the Auspicious One to Puõõaka, They hunger for sense pleasures because of gain, they engage in sacrifice, excited by the passion for existence. They have not crossed over birth and old age, I say.û

72 (1047) 35
ßIf those engaged in sacrifice have not crossed over, said venerable Puõõaka, birth and old age by their sacrifices, dear Sir, then just who in the world of the gods and men has crossed over birth and old age, dear Sir? I ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me.û

73 (1048) 36
ßThe one who has discerned far and near in the world, said the Auspicious One to Puõõaka, who has no turmoil anywhere in the world, who is peaceful, not incensed, not troubled, not yearning, he has crossed over birth and old age, I sayû

The Young Man Puõõaka's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Mettagå's Questions

 

74 (1049) 41
ßI ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me, said venerable Mettagå, I think you have true understanding, and a developed self - how have these countless kinds of suffering arisen for whoever is in the world?û

75 (1050) 42
ßYou asked me about the origin of suffering, Mettagå said the Auspicious One, as I know it I will declare it to you: Because of cleaving to a basis countless kinds of suffering originate for whoever is in the world.

76 (1051) 43
The foolish one, without wisdom, cleaves to a basis, and comes to suffering again and again, the fool. Therefore knowing this, do not cleave to a basis, seeing the birth and origin of suffering.û

77 (1052) 44
ßYou have proclaimed to us what we asked you about, said venerable Mettagå, another thing we ask, come on, please answer it: How do the wise ones cross over the flood of birth, old age, grief, and lamentation? Please explain this to me, O sage, for this Dhamma has been understood by you.û

78 (1053) 45
ßI shall proclaim the Dhamma to you, Mettagå said the Auspicious One, which is not hearsay in this world, which, having understood, and living mindfully, one can cross over clinging to the world.û

79 (1054) 46
ßI greatly rejoice in that supreme Dhamma, great seer, which, having understood, and living mindfully, one can cross over clinging to the world.û 

80 (1055) 47
ßWhatever you know, Mettagå said the Auspicious One, above, below, and across the middle, dispel the enjoyment of, and settling on, these things, and consciousness, and one will not remain in existence.

81 (1056) 48
Living in this way, mindful, and heedful, the bhikkhu who lives on after giving up loved things, can, being wise, give up birth, old age, grief, lamentation, and suffering right here.û

82 (1057) 49
ßI greatly rejoice in this, the great seer's word, said venerable Mettagå, O Gotama, well proclaimed is freedom from cleaving. Surely the Auspicious One gave up suffering, for this Dhamma has been understood by you.

83 (1058) 410
Surely they too can give up suffering, whomever you would continually advise, O sage, therefore having met the nàga, I revere him, perhaps the Auspicious One could continuously advise me!û

84 (1059) 411
ßYou should know the brahmin who has true understanding, Mettagå, said the Auspicious One, who has nothing, and is not clinging to sense existence, for he has surely crossed over the flood, without hindrance or doubt, he has crossed beyond.

85 (1060) 4-12
That man here who is wise, and has true understanding, who has released the shackle of repeated existence, he is craving-free, not troubled, not yearning - he has crossed over birth and old age, I say.û

The Young Man Mettagå's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Dhotaka's Questions

 

86 (1061) 5-1
ßI ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me, said venerable Dhotaka, I am waiting for a word of yours, great seer, having heard your utterance I could train for my own nibbàna.û

87 (1062) 5-2
ßThen you must become ardent, Dhotaka said the Auspicious One, being prudent and mindful right here, henceforth, after hearing this utterance you should train for your own nibbàna.û

88 (1063) 5-3
ßI see in the world with its gods and men, said venerable Dhotaka, a brahmin who moves about having nothing, therefore I revere you, All-Seeing Visionary, O Sakyan, please free me from my doubts.û

89 (1064) 5-4
ßI will not be able to free anyone in the world who is having doubts, Dhotaka, but knowing (nibbàna), the best dhamma, in this way you can cross over the flood.û

90 (1065) 5-5
ßBrahmin! Being compassionate teach, said venerable Dhotaka, the state of detachment that I should know, then I, being undisturbed, just like the sky, may live peaceful and independent right here.û

91 (1066) 5-6
ßI shall proclaim the peace to you, Dhotaka, said the Auspicious One, which is not hearsay here in the world, which, having understood, and living mindfully, one can cross over clinging to the world.û

92 (1067) 5-7
ßI greatly rejoice in that supreme peace, great seer, which, having understood, and living mindfully, one can cross over clinging to the world.û

93 (1068) 5-8
ßWhatever you know, Dhotaka, said the Auspicious One, above, below, and across the middle, having understood this is called a shackle in the world, you must not have craving for repeated existence.û

The Young Man Dhotaka's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Upasãva's Questions

 

94 (1069) 6-1
ßAlone, Sakyan, with nothing to depend on, said venerable Upasãva, I am not able to cross over the great flood. Please tell me of a support, All-Seeing Visionary, depending on which I can cross over this flood.û

95 (1070) 6-2
ßLooking to nothingness, and being mindful, Upasãva, said the Auspicious One, depending on nothing, cross over the flood. Having given up sense pleasures, abstaining from talk, day and night you must look for the end of suffering.û

96 (1071) 6-3
ßHe who is passionless regarding all sense pleasures, said venerable Upasãva, who is depending on nothingness, having given up all else, intent on the highest freedom which still has perception - will he remain there without going away?û

97 (1072) 6-4
ßHe who is passionless regarding all sense pleasures, Upasãva, said the Auspicious One, who is depending on nothingness, having given up all else, intent on the highest freedom which still has perception - he will remain there without going away.û 

98 (1073) 6-5
ßIf he remains there without going away for a great number of years, All-Seeing Visionary, will he become cool and free right there, or will the consciousness of such a one fall away?û

99 (1074) 6-6
ßAs a flame overthrown by the force of the wind, Upasãva, said the Auspicious One, goes to rest and can no longer be discerned, just so the sage free from the mental body goes to rest and can no longer be discerned.û

100 (1075) 6-7
ßThe one who has come to rest, is he then nothing? said venerable Upasãva, or is he actually eternally healthy? Please explain this to me, O sage, for this Dhamma has been understood by you.û

101 (1076) 6-8
ßThere is no measure of the one who has come to rest, Upasãva, said the Auspicious One, there is nothing by which they can speak of him, when everything has been completely removed, all the pathways for speech are also completely removed.û

The Young Man Upasãva's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Nanda's Questions

 

102 (1077) 7-1
ß`There are sages in the world', said venerable Nanda, that is what people say - how is this so? Do they say he is a sage because he has knowledge Or because of the life he leads?û

103 (1078) 7-2
ßNot by view, tradition, or knowledge, do the skilful say one is a sage here, Nanda, whoever lives without company, not troubled, not yearning, they are sages, I say.û

104 (1079) 7-3
ßWhoever (among) these ascetics and brahmins, said venerable Nanda, say that purity is through what is seen or heard, say that purity is through virtue and practices, say that purity is through countless (other) ways, Auspicious One, perhaps those living restrained in this way have crossed over birth and old age, dear Sir? I ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me.û

105 (1080) 7-4
ßWhoever (among) these ascetics and brahmins, Nanda, said the Auspicious One, say that purity is through what is seen or heard, say that purity is through virtue and practices, say that purity is through countless (other) ways, although they are living restrained in this way, they have not crossed over bith and old age, I say.û

106 (1081) 7-5/6
ßWhoever (among) these ascetics and brahmins, said venerable Nanda, say that purity is through what is seen or heard, say that purity is through virtue and practices, say that purity is through countless (other) ways, if you say these sages have not crossed the flood, then just who in the world of the gods and men have crossed over birth and old age, dear Sir? I ask you, Auspicious One, please tell this to me.û

107 (1082) 7-7
ßI do not say that all ascetics and brahmins, Nanda, said the Auspicious One, are enveloped in birth and old age: whoever here has given up (reliance on) what is seen, heard, or sensed, and virtue and practices, and has also given up all the countless (other) ways, who, by fully knowing craving, are pollutant-free - I say those men have crossed over the flood.û

108 (1083) 7-8
I greatly rejoice in the great seer's word, Gotama, well-proclaimed is freedom from cleaving! Whoever here has given up (reliance on) what is seen, heard, or sensed, and virtue and practices, and has also given up all the countless (other) ways, who, by fully knowing craving, are pollutant-free - I also say they have crossed over the flood.û

The Young Man Nanda's Questions are Finished

The Young Man Hemaka's Questions

109 (1084) 8-1
ßThose who explained (things) to me in the past, said venerable Hemaka, before Gotama's teaching, saying `it was so, and so it will be', all that was just hearsay, all that just increased my thoughts, and I did not delight therein.

110 (1085) 8-2
You must explain the Dhamma to me, O sage, and the destruction of craving, which, having understood, and living mindfully, one can cross over clinging to the world.û

111 (1086) 8-3
ßIn regard to likeable forms here seen, heard, sensed, and cognized, Hemaka, the dispelling of desire and passion (for them) - this is the undying nibbàna.

112 (1087) 8-4
Knowing this, those who are mindful, who have become still in this very life, those who are always peaceful, have crossed over clinging to the world.û

The Young Man Hemaka's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Todeyya's Questions

 

113 (1088) 9-1
ßHe who has no in-dwelling sense desires, said venerable Todeyya, he in whom no craving is found, he who crossed beyond doubts, what kind of freedom is there for him?û

114 (1089) 9-2
ßHe who has no in-dwelling sense desires, said the Auspicious One, he in whom no craving is found, he who has crossed beyond doubts, there is no further freedom for him.û

115 (1090) 9-3
ßIs he without yearning, or is he still yearning? said venerable Todeyya, Is he wise, or is he still acquiring wisdom? I would know just what the sage is like, Sakyan: Explain that to me, All-Seeing Visionary.û

116 (1091) 9-4
ßHe is without yearning, he is not still yearning. He is wise, he is not still acquiring wisdom. Know that the sage is just like this, Todeyya: he has nothing, and is not clinging to sense existence.û

The Young Man Todeyya's Questions are Finished

The Young Man Kappa's Questions

 

117 (1092) 10-1
ßFor those standing in the middle of a lake, said venerable Kappa, when a fearful flood has arisen, for those overcome by old age and death, speak (about) an island, dear Sir, you must explain an island to me, so there will be no more after this.û

118 (1093) 10-2
ßFor those standing in the middle of a lake, Kappa, said the Auspicious One, when a fearful flood has arisen, for those overcome by old age and death, I speak (about) an island, Kappa:

119 (1094) 10-3
having nothing, no attachment, this is the island with nothing beyond, this is called nibbàna, I say, the end of old age and death.

120 (1095) 10-4
Knowing this, those who are mindful, who have become still in this very life, come not under Màra's control, they are not servants to Màra.û

The Young Man Kappa's Questions are Finished

The Young Man Jatukaõõã's Questions

 

121 (1096) 11-1
ßHaving heard of a hero who has no desire for sense pleasures, said venerable Jatukaõõã, I came to ask that desireless one who is beyond the flood, please speak of the state of peace, conascent-eye, as it really is, Auspicious One, please tell me this.

122 (1097) 11-2
the Auspicious One moves about having vanquished sense desires, as the splendid sun (vanquishes) the earth with its splendour. To me of little wisdom, O one of great wisdom, explain the Dhamma so that I may know the complete giving up of birth and old age here.û

123 (1098) 11-3
ßRemove the greed for sense pleasures, Jatukaõõã, said the Auspicious One, having seen there is safety in renunciation; let there be nothing found in you that has been taken up or put down.

124 (1099) 11-4
You should erase that which is past, and have nothing for the future, if you take up nothing in the present you will live peacefully.

125 (1100) 11-5
For one altogether without greed for name-and-form, O brahmin, no pollutants are found whereby he can come under Death's control.û

The Young Man Jatukaõõã's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Bhadràvudha's Questions

126 (1101) 12-1
ßThe home-leaver, the craving-cutter, the unmoved one, said venerable Bhadràvudha, the enjoyment-leaver, the flood-crosser, the free one, the speculation-leaver, the intelligent one - (him) I beg, after hearing the nàga, they will go away from here.

127 (1102) 12-2
Various people have come from the countries, and are waiting for your saying, O hero, you must explain (the Dhamma) properly to them, for this Dhamma has been understood by you.û

128 (1103) 12-3

129 (1104) 12-4
Therefore knowing (this), the mindful monk should not be attached to anything in the whole world, seeing that with what is called attachment to clinging, these people are clinging to the realm of Death.û

The Young Man Bhadràvudha's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Udaya's Questions

130 (1105) 13-1
ßTo the meditator who sits dust-free, said venerable Udaya, with duty-done, pollutant-free, who is perfect in all things, I have come in need with a question: please tell of freedom through knowledge, the breaking up of ignorance.û

131 (1106) 13-2
ßThe giving up of sensual desire, Udaya, said the Auspicious One, and of sorrow, (these) two, the dispelling of sloth, and the constraint of worry (about wrong-doing),

132 (1107) 13-3
purity through equanimity and mindfulness, preceded by thought of the Dhamma, this I call freedom through knowledge, the breaking up of ignorance.û

133 (1108) 13-4
ßBy what is the world fettered? said venerable Udaya, By what does it roam about? By completely giving up what thing is there what is called nibbàna?û

134 (1109) 13-5
ßThe world is fettered by enjoyment, Udaya, said the Auspicious One, it roams about through reflections. By completely giving up craving there is what is called nibbàna.û

135 (1110) 13-6
For he who lives mindfully, how is it consciousness ceases? We have come to ask the Auspicious One, therefore may we hear your word.û

136 (1111) 13-7
ßWithout rejoicing over feeling on the inside or outside - for he who lives mindfully in this way, consciousness ceases.û

The Young Man Udaya's Questions are Finished

 

The Young Man Posàla's Questions

137 (1112) 14-1
ß(To) the one who points out the past, said venerable Posàla, the unmoved one, who has cut off doubt, who is perfect in everything, I have come in need with a question:

138 (1113) 14-2
For the one in whom perception of form has ended, (who), on the inside and outside is seeing `there is nothing whatsoever', I ask about (his) knowledge, Sakyan, how is such a one led further?û

139 (1114) 14-3
ßAll the stations of consciousness, Posàla, said the Auspicious One, the Realised One knows, he knows where that one stands, what he is intent on, what he is going towards.

140 (1115) 14-4
Having known the origin of nothingness, and that enjoyment is called a fetter, knowing deeply that it is so, and then having insight into this: this is real knowedge for him, for the brahmin who is accomplished.û

(Verses in Praise of the Way to the Beyond)

This was said by the Auspicious One while living among the Magadhans at the Pàsàõaka shrine. The sixteen attendent brahmins requested, asked, and asked, and he answered the questions. If, knowing the meaning of each question, knowing the Dhamma, he would practice in conformity with the Dhamma, he would surely go beyond old age and death. It is said these teachings go to the beyond, therefore this presentation of the Dhamma is indeed called The Way to the Beyond.

149 (1124) 1
Ajita, Tissa Metteyya, Puõõaka, also Mettagå, Dhotaka, and Upasãva, Nanda, and also Hemaka,

150 (1125) 2
the two: Todeyya, and Kappa, and Jatukaõõã, the one who is wise, Bhadràvudha, and Udaya, also the brahmin Posàla, Mogharàja, the intelligent one, and the great seer Pingiya.

151 (1126) 3
These went to the Buddha, to the seer with perfect conduct, asking subtle questions, they went to the Buddha who is best.

152 (1127) 4
The Buddha answered as it truly is the questions that they asked, and by answering the questions the sage satisfied those brahmins.

153 (1128) 5
They, being satisfied by the Visionary, the Buddha, the kinsman of the sun, lived the spiritual life under the one-with-excellent-wisdom.

154 (1129) 6
The Buddha taught like this (in response) to the questions one by one, he who practices like this can go from here to the beyond.

155 (1130) 7
From here to the beyond he can go by developing the supreme path. This is the path for going to the beyond, therefore it is called The Way to the Beyond.
