6: The Chapter (Including the Discourse) about the Congenitally Blind

 

1: The Discourse about the Relinquishment of the Life Process

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Vesàlã, in the Gabled Hall in the Great Wood. Then the Auspicious One, having dressed in the morning time, after picking up his bowl and robe, entered Vesàlã for alms. After walking for alms in Vesàlã, when he had returned from the alms-round after the meal, he addressed venerable ânanda, (saying): ßTake up the sitting mat, ânanda, we will go to the Càpàla shrine to dwell for the day.û

ßYes, reverend Sir,û said venerable ânanda, and after replying to the Auspicious One, and taking the sitting mat, he followed along close behind the Auspicious One. Then the Auspicious One went to the Càpàla shrine, and after going, he sat down on the prepared seat. While sat the Auspicious One addressed venerable ânanda, (saying): ßDelightful, ânanda, is Vesàlã, delightful is the Udena shrine, delightful is the Gotamaka shrine, delightful is the Sattamba shrine, delightful is the Bahuputta shrine, delightful is the Sàrandada shrine, delightful is the Càpàla shrine. Whoever has developed, ânanda, made much of, carried on, established, maintained, augmented, and properly instigated the four paths to power, could, if he wanted, remain for the life-span or for what is left of the life-span. The Realised One has developed, ânanda, made much of, carried on, established, maintained, augmented, and properly instigated the four paths to power. If he wanted, ânanda, the Realised One could remain for the life-span or for what is left of the life-span.û

But venerable ânanda, despite such a gross hint being made by the Auspicious One, such a gross suggestion being made, was unable to penetrate it, and did not beg the Auspicious One, (saying): ßMay the Auspicious One remain, reverend Sir, for the life-span; may the Fortunate One remain for the life-span, for the benefit of many people, for the happiness of many people, out of compassion for the world, for the welfare, benefit, and happiness of devas and men,û like one whose mind was possessed by Màra.

For a second time the Auspicious One addressed venerable ânanda, (saying): ßDelightful, ânanda, is Vesàlã, delightful is the Udena shrine, delightful is the Gotamaka shrine, delightful is the Sattamba shrine, delightful is the Bahuputta shrine, delightful is the Sàrandada shrine, delightful is the Càpàla shrine. The Realised One has developed, ânanda, made much of, carried on, established, maintained, augmented, and properly instigated the four paths to power. If he wanted, ânanda, the Realised One could remain for the life-span or for what is left of the life-span.û

But venerable ânanda, despite such a gross hint being made by the Auspicious One, such a gross suggestion being made, was unable to penetrate it, and did not beg the Auspicious One, (saying): ßMay the Auspicious One remain, reverend Sir, for the life-span, may the Fortunate One remain for the life-span, for the benefit of many people, for the happiness of many people, out of compassion for the world, for the welfare, benefit, and happiness of devas and men,û like one whose mind was possessed by Màra.

 For a third time the Auspicious One addressed venerable ânanda, (saying): ßDelightful, ânanda, is Vesàlã, delightful is the Udena shrine, delightful is the Gotamaka shrine, delightful is the Sattamba shrine, delightful is the Bahuputta shrine, delightful is the Sàrandada shrine, delightful is the Càpàla shrine. But venerable ânanda, despite such a gross hint being made by the Auspicious One, such a gross suggestion being made, was unable to penetrate it, and did not beg the Auspicious One, (saying): ßMay the Auspicious One remain, reverend Sir, for the life-span, may the Fortunate One remain for the life-span, for the benefit of many people, for the happiness of many people, out of compassion for the world, for the welfare, benefit, and happiness of devas and men,û like one whose mind was possessed by Màra.

ßYes, reverend Sir,û said venerable ânanda, and after replying to the Auspicious One, rising from his seat, worshipping and circumambulating the Auspicious One, he sat down not far away at the root of a certain tree.

But at present, reverend Sir, the Auspicious One's spiritual life is prosperous and successful, well spread out, possessed by many, become great, it is well explained amongst devas and men. May the Auspicious One attain complete emancipation, reverend Sir, may the Fortunate One attain complete emancipation, now is the time, reverend Sir, for the Auspicious One's complete emancipation.û

When that was said the Auspicious One said this to Màra the Bad: ßYou should have little concern, Bad One, in no long time the Realised One will become completely emancipated, after three months have passed from now, the Realised One will attain complete emancipation.û

Then at the Càpàla shrine the Auspicious One, mindfully and clearly knowing, relinquished the life process. With the relinquishment of the life process by the Auspicious One there was a great earthquake, and a fearful, horrifying crash of the devas' (thunder) drum.

Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßMeasureable and immeasureable continuity,
And the process of continuation the Sage relinquished.
Internally content, and concentrated,
He broke continuity of self like a coat of mail.û

 

2: The Discourse about Those with Knotted Hair

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Sàvatthã, at the Eastern Monastery in Migàra's mother's mansion. Then at that time the Auspicious One having risen from seclusion in the evening time, was sitting outside the doorway.

Then the Kosalan King Pasenadi went to the Auspicious One, and after going and worshipping the Auspicious One, he sat down on one side. Then at that time seven knotted hair ascetics, seven of those knot-free (i.e. Jainas), seven naked ascetics, seven one-cloakers, and seven wanderers, with their nails, armpit-hair, and body hair grown long, having taken up their various requisites, were passing by not far away from the Auspicious One.

The Kosalan King Pasenadi saw those seven knotted hair ascetics, seven of those knot-free, seven naked ascetics, seven one-cloakers, and seven wanderers, with their nails, armpit-hair, and body hair grown long, who, having taken up their various requisites, were passing by not far away from the Auspicious One. And having seen (them), after rising from his seat, arranging his outer robe on one shoulder, placing his right knee-cap on the ground, towards the place where those seven knotted hair ascetics, seven of those knot-free, seven naked ascetics, seven one-cloakers, and seven wanderers were, raising his hands in respectful salutation, he three times announced his name, (saying): ßReverend Sirs, I am the Kosalan King Pasenadi.û

Then the Kosalan King Pasenadi, not long after those seven knotted hair ascetics, seven of those knot-free, seven naked ascetics, seven one-cloakers, and seven wanderers had gone, went to the Auspicious One, and after going and worshipping the Auspicious One, he sat down on one side, While sat on one side the Kosalan King Pasenadi said this to the Auspicious One: ßAmong those in the world reverend Sir, who are Worthy Ones, or have entered the path to Worthiness, these are some of them.û

ßBut this is hard to know, Great King, by you, a householder, partaking of sense pleasures, by one dwelling in a sleeping place crowded with sons, by one enjoying sandalwood from Kàsi, by one wearing garlands, scents, and creams, by one accepting gold and silver: `These are Worthy Ones, or these have entered the path to Worthiness,'

Through living together, Great King, one can understand (a person's) virtue, and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.

Through having dealings together, Great King, one can understand (a person's) purity,  and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.

Through misfortunes, Great King, one can understand (a person's) stamina,  and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.

Through discussion, Great King, one can understand (a person's) wisdom,  and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.û

ßIt is wonderful, reverend Sir, it is marvellous, reverend Sir, how well spoken that was, reverend Sir, by the Auspicious One: `But this is hard to know, Great King, by you, a householder, partaking of sense pleasures, by one dwelling in a sleeping place crowded with sons, by one enjoying sandalwood from Kàsi, by one wearing garlands, scents, and creams, by one accepting gold and silver: ßThese are Worthy, or these have entered the path to Worthiness,û

Through living together, Great King, one can understand (a person's) virtue, and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.

Through having dealings together, Great King, one can understand (a person's) purity, and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.

Through misfortunes, Great King, one can understand (a person's) stamina, and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.

Through discussion, Great King, one can understand (a person's) wisdom, and that after a long time, and not after a short one, by one applying the mind, not by one not applying the mind, by one with wisdom, not by one with poor wisdom.'

These are my men, imposters and spies, reverend Sir, who are returning after spying on the country, for first they have gone down (to the country), and afterwards I will go down. Now these, reverend Sir, having removed dust and dirt, being bathed, well annointed, with trimmed hair and beards, dressed in clean clothes, and provided with and possessing the five strands of sensual pleasures, will amuse themselves.û

Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßOne should not endeavour in all circumstances,    one should not be another's man,
One should not live depending on another,    one should not live trading in Dhamma.û

 

3: The Discourse about Reflecting

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Sàvatthã, at Anàthapiõóika's monastery in Jeta's Wood. Then at that time the Auspicious One was sitting reflecting on his own elimination of countless bad, unwholesome things, and how through development countless wholesome things had come to fulfilment.

Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßBefore it was, then it wasn't,    before it wasn't, then it was,
It was not, and it will not be,    and at present it is not found.û

 

4: The First Discourse about the Various Sectarians

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Sàvatthã, at Anàthapiõóika's monastery in Jeta's Wood. Then at that time many and various sectarians, ascetics, bràhmaõas, and wanderers were living at Sàvatthã, having various views, various likings, various tendencies, subject to dependence on various views, (such as):

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe world is eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe world is not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe world is finite - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe world is infinite - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThat which is soul, that is (also) the body - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe soul is one thing, the body is another thing, - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe individual exists after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe individual does not exist after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

Some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe individual exists and does not exist after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe individual neither exists nor does not exist after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

They lived contending, quarelling, disputing, attacking each other with sharp tongues, (saying): ßSuch is Dhamma, such is not Dhamma; such is not Dhamma, such is Dhamma.û

Then many monks, having dressed in the morning time, after picking up their bowl and robe, entered Sàvatthã for alms. After walking for alms in Sàvatthã, while returning from the alms-round after the meal, they went to the Auspicious One, and after going and worshipping the Auspicious One, they sat down on one side.

While sat on one side those monks said this to the Auspicious One: ßHere, reverend Sir, many and various sectarians, ascetics, bràhmaõas, and wanderers are living at Sàvatthã, having various views, various likings, various tendencies, subject to dependence on various views, (such as):

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The world is eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The world is not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The world is finite - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The world is infinite - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `That which is soul, that is (also) the body - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The soul is one thing, the body is another thing, - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The individual exists after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The individual does not exist after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

Some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The individual exists and does not exist after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who are of this argument, this view: `The individual neither exists nor does not exist after death - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.'

They live contending, quarelling, disputing, attacking each other with sharp tongues, (saying): `Such is Dhamma, such is not Dhamma; such is not Dhamma, such is Dhamma.' û

ßWanderers from other sects, monks, are blind, without vision, they do not know what is good, they do not know what is not good, they do not know what is Dhamma, they do not know what is not Dhamma. They, not knowing what is good, not knowing what is not good, not knowing what is Dhamma, not knowing what is not Dhamma, live contending, quarelling, disputing, attacking each other with sharp tongues, (saying): `Such is Dhamma, such is not Dhamma; such is not Dhamma, such is Dhamma.'

Formerly, monks, in this Sàvatthã there was a certain King. Then that King, monks, addressed a certain man, (saying): `Please go, dear Sir, and as many of those congenitally blind as there are in Sàvatthã assemble them in one place.'

`Yes, your Majesty', said that man, monks, and after replying to the King, after grabbing all of those congenitally blind as there were in Sàvatthã, he went to the King, and after going he said this to the King: `As many of those congenitally blind as there are in Sàvatthã, your Majesty, have assembled.'

`Then show an elephant, I say, to those congenitally blind.'

`Yes, your Majesty', said that man, monks, and after replying to the King, he showed an elephant to those congenitally blind, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!'

To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's head, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's ear, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's tusk, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's trunk, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's body, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's leg, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the elephant's thigh, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the top of the elephant's tail, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!' To some of those congenitally blind he showed the tip of the elephant's tail, (saying): `Such is an elephant, blind men!'

Then, monks, that man, having shown the elephant to those congenitally blind, went to the King, and after going he said this to the King: `Those congenitally blind have seen the elephant, your Majesty, now is the time for whatever you are thinking, your Majesty.' Then that king, monks, went to the blind men, and after going he said this to those blind men: `Did you see the elephant, blind men?'

`Yes, your Majesty, we did see the elephant.'

`Speak, blind men, (and say): ßSuch is an elephant.û ' 

Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's head, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a pot.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's ear, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a winnowing fan.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's tusk, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a ploughshare.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's trunk, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a plough-pole.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's body, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a store-houseû Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's leg, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a pillar.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the elephant's thighs, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a mortar.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the top of the elephant's tail, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a pestle.û Those congenitally blind, monks, who had seen the tip of the elephant's tail, said this: `Such is an elephant, your Majesty, he is like a broom,û and they, (saying): `Such is an elephant, such is not an elephant; such is not an elephant, such is an elephant,' hit each other with their fists, and with that, monks, the King was pleased.

Just so, monks, wanderers from other sects  are blind, without vision, they do not know what is good, they do not know what is not good, they do not know what is Dhamma, they do not know what is not Dhamma. They, not knowing what is good, not knowing what is not good, not knowing what is Dhamma, not knowing what is not Dhamma, live contending, quarelling, disputing, attacking each other with sharp tongues, (saying): `Such is Dhamma, such is not Dhamma; such is not Dhamma, such is Dhamma.' û

Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßSome ascetics and bràhmaõas, it seems, are attached to these (views),
Having grasped ahold of it, they dispute,    (like) people who see (only) one side.û

 

5: The Second Discourse about the Various Sectarians

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Sàvatthã, at Anàthapiõóika's monastery in Jeta's Wood. Then at that time many and various sectarians, ascetics, bràhmaõas, and wanderers were living at Sàvatthã, having various views, various likings, various tendencies, subject to dependence on various views, (such as):-

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are eternal and not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are neither eternal nor not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are made by oneself - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are made by another - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are made by oneself and by another - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßThe self and the world are neither made by oneself nor by another, but have arisen fortuitously - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

Some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßPleasure and pain, the self and the world are eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßPleasure and pain, the self and the world are not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßPleasure and pain, the self and the world are eternal and not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

There are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßPleasure and pain, the self and the world are neither eternal nor not eternal - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

But there are some ascetics and bràhmaõas who were of this argument, this view: ßPleasure and pain, the self and the world are made by oneself - this alone is the truth, (all) else is foolish.û

8: The Discourse about the Courtesan

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Ràjagaha, at the Squirrel's Feeding Place in Bamboo Wood. Then at that time in Ràjagaha there were two gangs, whose minds were bound to, and impassioned with, a certain courtesan. Contending, quarelling, and disputing, they attacked each other with their hands, attacked with clods of earth, attacked with sticks, attacked with swords, and there (and then) they underwent death, and pain like unto death.

Then many monks, having dressed in the morning time, after picking up their bowls and robes, entered Ràjagaha for alms, and after walking for alms in Ràjagaha, while returning from the alms-round after the meal, went to the Auspicious One, and after going and worshipping the Auspicious One, they sat down on one side. While sat on one side those monks said this to the Auspicious One: ßHere, reverend Sir, in Ràjagaha there are two gangs, whose minds are bound to, and impassioned with, a certain courtesan. Contending, quarelling, and disputing, they attack each other with their hands, attack with clods of earth, attack with sticks, attack with swords, and there (and then) they undergo death, and pain like unto death.û

Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßWhat has been attained, and what can be attained - these two are strewn with dust, for that miserable one in (wrong) training. Those who hold the training rules as the essence, or virtue and practices, (right) livelihood, celibacy, and attendance as the essence - this is one end.

Those who say this: `There is no fault in sense pleasuresû - this is the second end. Thus these two ends promote the cemetery grounds, and the cemetery grounds promote (wrong) view.

Not having understood these two ends, some get stuck, some go too far. But for those who have understood these, who were not in that, and because of that do not conceive (a conceit) - there is no Cycle (of saüsàra) to be assigned for them.û

 

9: The Discourse about (Running) Beyond

Thus I heard:

at one time the Auspicious One was dwelling near Sàvatthã, at Anàthapiõóika's monastery in Jeta's Wood. Then at that time the Auspicious One, in the darkness of the night, was sitting in the open air, while the oil-lamps were burning. Then at that time many moths rushing and falling down into those oil lamps, were coming to grief, were coming to ruin.

The Auspicious One saw those many moths (who), rushing and falling down into those oil lamps, were coming to grief, were coming to ruin.

Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßThey run beyond, not coming to the essential,
They cause new and fresh bonds to increase,
Like moths that fall into the lamp,
Some have settled thus on what is seen (or) heard.û

 

10: The Discourse about the Arising of the Realised One

Thus I heard:

ßJust so, ânanda, for as long as the Realised Ones, ânanda, do not arise in the world, the Worthy Ones, the Perfect Sambuddhas, for that long the wanderers from other sects are venerated, respected, revered, honoured, esteemed, and in receipt of robes, almsfood, dwellings, and medicinal requisites to help when sick. Then the Auspicious One, having understood the significance of it, on that occasion uttered this exalted utterance:

ßThe glow worm shines forth
for as long as the light-maker (the sun) does not rise,
But when the brilliant one (the sun) has risen,
The (glow worm's) light is destroyed, and does not shine forth.

The reasoners are not purified, nor are their disciples,
